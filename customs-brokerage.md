## What is a customs broker?

A customs broker ensures that your goods clear customs during the shipment process by being the intermediary between you and customs. They inform the shipper of the necessary documentation to have the goods cleared and prepare and submit it to get clearance from customs.

A customs broker is essential because an issue at customs can delay your shipment.\[/vc\_column\_text\]\[vc\_column\_text css=”” el\_id=”2″\]

## Customs bond

For most shipments you may also be required to purchase a customs bond (if you don’t already have one). This can either be a single-entry bond (SEB) or a continuous bond. The better, more cost-effective option for you will depend on how many times you expect to ship in the next 12 months.

The cost of an SEB is dependent on the value of your goods, typically 0.5% of their total worth, with a minimum charge of $50. For shipments arriving by ocean, a customer with a single-entry bond will also need to purchase an ISF (Import Security Filing) bond separately for a flat fee of approximately $75.

A continuous bond typically costs $500 and is valid for 12 months from the date of purchase, encompassing any and all shipments into the US registered to the company holding the bond. No further charges are required.

Therefore, if you are shipping more than five times a year, it may be cheaper long term to purchase a continuous bond, especially as it covers additional bond requirements.

Additionally, many users have reported that single entry bonds are more likely to be inspected at customs, which can incur more charges; less customs holds issues have been reported for continuous bonds.\[/vc\_column\_text\]\[vc\_column\_text css=”” el\_id=”4″\]

## Understanding HTS Codes

The Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) code is a standardized numerical system used to classify traded products in international commerce. This system, developed by the World Customs Organization, consists of 6 to 10-digit codes that categorize thousands of products based on their material composition, function, and industry.

HTS codes are essential for determining customs duties, taxes, and regulations applicable to imported goods. The first six digits represent the international HS code, while additional digits provide country-specific classifications. In the United States, HTS codes contain 10 digits, with the last four digits specific to U.S. import requirements.

Accurate HTS classification is crucial for businesses engaged in international trade, as incorrect codes can result in overpayment of duties, shipment delays, or penalties from customs authorities. Companies must carefully analyze product characteristics, materials, and intended use to determine the correct classification.

Importers can find HTS codes through the U.S. International Trade Commission’s online database or by consulting with customs brokers and trade specialists. Understanding and properly applying HTS codes helps ensure regulatory compliance, optimize duty costs, and facilitate smooth customs clearance for international shipments, making it a fundamental component of successful global trade operations.\[/vc\_column\_text\]\[vc\_separator\]\[vc\_column\_text\][Back to Top](#top)\[/vc\_column\_text\]\[/vc\_column\]\[/vc\_row\]
